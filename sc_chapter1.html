Stock & Lambert Definition
A supply chain is a system of organizations, people, activities, information, and resources involved in moving a product or service from supplier to customer. It encompasses every step from raw material extraction to final delivery — and everything in between.
The Three Flows — Always Running Simultaneously
Product flow → Physical goods moving downstream, from suppliers toward customers.
Information flow ↔ Orders, forecasts, shipment data, demand signals — moving in both directions.
Financial flow ← Payments, invoices, credit terms — moving upstream toward suppliers.
Why It Matters
Supply chain costs account for 50–70% of revenue in most manufacturing industries. A company that manages its supply chain well can offer lower prices, faster delivery, and higher reliability than a competitor with the same product — and that is a permanent competitive advantage.

Chapter 1 · Perspectives
How Different People See the Supply Chain
Supply chain management means something different depending on where you sit in an organization. Click each perspective to see how the same supply chain looks through different eyes.
🏭
Operations View
How do we produce efficiently and meet demand?
Operations managers see the supply chain as a production system. Their focus: minimize waste, reduce cycle time, maintain quality, and keep lines running. They worry about capacity, scheduling, and throughput — the internal efficiency of the factory or facility.
💰
Financial View
How do we reduce working capital and improve cash flow?
CFOs and finance teams see the supply chain as a balance sheet. Inventory is cash tied up in product. Accounts payable terms affect liquidity. Long lead times increase risk. They push for lower inventory, faster turns, and supply chain financing programs that extend payment terms without harming suppliers.
📦
Logistics View
How do we move product efficiently from A to B?
Logistics professionals see the supply chain as a transportation and warehousing network. Their focus: carrier selection, routing, freight costs, warehouse layout, and on-time delivery. They are the operational backbone — without them, product does not physically reach customers.
🤝
Procurement View
How do we source the right inputs at the right cost?
Procurement managers see the supply chain as a supplier portfolio. Their focus: supplier qualification, contract terms, price negotiation, supplier risk, and sourcing strategy. Should we single-source or multi-source? Domestic or global? Build or buy? These decisions shape the structure of the entire upstream network.
👤
Customer View
Did I get what I ordered, when I needed it, in good condition?
Customers do not see the supply chain — they see its outcome. They measure service level (did it arrive on time?), order accuracy (was it correct?), and product quality (was it undamaged?). The entire purpose of supply chain management is to deliver a result the customer values.
🌐
Strategic View
How does our supply chain create competitive advantage?
Executives see the supply chain as a strategic asset. Apple's supply chain allows it to launch 100M iPhones in 6 weeks. Walmart's allows it to price below every competitor. Zara's lets it turn a runway trend into store product in 15 days. The supply chain is not just operations — it is strategy made physical.
The Integration Imperative
Each perspective above is legitimate — and each one, if pursued in isolation, will sub-optimize the system. Operations running at full capacity without regard for demand creates excess inventory. Procurement driving the lowest cost without regard for supplier resilience creates vulnerability. Supply chain management is the discipline that integrates all of these perspectives so the whole system performs better than the sum of its parts.
Chapter 1 · History
How Supply Chain Management Evolved
Supply chain management did not appear overnight. It developed over 80 years as companies faced new pressures — war, globalization, technology, and crises — and responded with new ideas. The timeline below shows the turning points that define the field you are studying.
1940s
Wartime Logistics
World War II forced the US military to coordinate the movement of millions of tons of equipment, food, and fuel across two oceans. This was the first time logistics was treated as a strategic discipline rather than a necessary burden.
1950s–60s
Physical Distribution Management
Companies begin managing transportation, warehousing, and inventory as a connected system rather than separate cost centers. The term "physical distribution" enters the business lexicon.
1970s
Materials Management & Just-in-Time
Toyota develops the Just-in-Time production system — delivering parts exactly when needed, eliminating buffer inventory. This forces manufacturers to think about supplier coordination as a core competency, not an afterthought.
1982
The Term "Supply Chain Management" Is Born
Keith Oliver at Booz Allen Hamilton coins the phrase "supply chain management" in an interview with the Financial Times — formalizing the idea that upstream suppliers and downstream customers should be managed as a single integrated system.
1990s
Globalization & ERP Systems
Companies offshore manufacturing to Asia, creating complex global networks. SAP, Oracle, and other ERP systems emerge to manage information across these networks. The supply chain becomes global — and exponentially more complex.
2000s
E-Commerce Transforms Fulfillment
Amazon demonstrates that customers will pay for speed and convenience. The rise of e-commerce forces every retailer to redesign their supply chain around direct-to-consumer fulfillment — a fundamental shift in distribution strategy.
2011
Japan Earthquake — Resilience Becomes Priority
A single earthquake and tsunami exposes how vulnerable hyper-efficient global supply chains are to disruption. Toyota, Nissan, and hundreds of others lose months of production. The industry begins investing seriously in supply chain risk management.
2020–21
COVID-19 — The Supply Chain Enters Public Consciousness
For the first time, consumers experience supply chain disruption personally — empty shelves, semiconductor shortages, 18-month appliance wait times. The pandemic reveals the fragility of just-in-time systems and triggers a global rethink of supply chain strategy.
Today
Resilience, Sustainability & Digital Transformation
Modern supply chain management focuses on three simultaneous imperatives: building resilient networks that can absorb shocks, reducing environmental impact, and deploying digital tools — sensors, analytics, and automation — to create visibility and agility across complex global networks.
Chapter 1 · Societal Issues
Supply Chains Shape Society
Supply chains are not just business systems — they are the infrastructure of modern life. When they work, they raise living standards, create jobs, and deliver medicines. When they fail, the consequences are visible at every grocery shelf and gas pump.

Key Societal Issues
👷
Labor Standards
Global supply chains often source from countries with weaker labor protections. Companies face pressure — and legal obligations — to audit their suppliers for forced labor, unsafe conditions, and child labor.
🌱
Environmental Sustainability
Freight transportation accounts for 8% of global CO₂ emissions. Companies are under growing pressure to measure and reduce the carbon footprint of their supply chains — from packaging to last-mile delivery.
🍎
Food Security
Modern food supply chains feed 8 billion people. Disruptions — drought, disease, logistics failure — can cause food shortages and price spikes that fall hardest on the poorest populations.
💊
Pharmaceutical Access
Cold chain failures in developing countries destroy 25% of vaccines before they reach patients. Supply chain design directly determines whether life-saving medicines reach the people who need them.
🏘️
Economic Development
Integration into global supply chains is the primary path by which developing countries generate export income, create manufacturing jobs, and build economic capacity.
🛡️
National Security
Governments now treat supply chains as a national security issue. Dependence on foreign suppliers for semiconductors, pharmaceuticals, and rare earth minerals is a vulnerability that policy is increasingly designed to address.
Chapter 1 · The Framework
The Stock & Lambert SCM Model
Stock & Lambert argue that supply chain management rests on three interdependent pillars. Understanding all three — and how they interact — is the foundation of the course. Click each pillar to expand.
01
Supply Chain Network Structure
Who are the members and how are they connected?
The network structure defines which companies are part of your supply chain and how they relate to each other. This includes decisions about how many tiers your network has, which suppliers you work with directly versus through intermediaries, and how many facilities you need. Network structure decisions are long-term and expensive to change — getting them right is a strategic imperative.
02
Business Processes
What activities create value for customers?
Business processes are the activities that actually create value: customer relationship management, demand management, order fulfillment, manufacturing flow, supplier relationship management, product development, and returns management. Stock & Lambert identify 8 core supply chain processes. Managing them requires coordination across companies — which is what makes SCM difficult.
03
Management Components
What tools and practices govern how the network operates?
Management components are the systems and practices that make integration possible: planning and control systems, work structure, organizational structure, product flow management, information flow management, management methods, power and leadership structures, and culture. These components are what separate companies that talk about integration from those that actually achieve it.
Why Integration Is Hard
Every company in a supply chain has its own goals, metrics, and incentives. A supplier wants high prices and long contracts. A manufacturer wants low prices and flexibility. A retailer wants perfect service and vendor-managed inventory. Integration does not happen automatically — it requires deliberate design, shared information, aligned incentives, and trust built over time. That is why supply chain management is a strategic discipline, not just a set of operational tools.
The Bullwhip Effect — Why Integration Failures Are Costly
When companies do not share information, demand signals distort as they move upstream. A retailer sees a 5% demand increase and orders 15% more. The distributor sees that 15% and orders 30% more. The manufacturer sees 30% and increases production 60%. This amplification — called the bullwhip effect — wastes billions annually in excess inventory, overtime, and markdowns. Information sharing eliminates it.
